How this set was built:
This paper was reverse-engineered from e-con systems' past 'Puzzles Question Paper' sets (35–45 minute rounds, ~6–15 questions) and the puzzle/brain-teaser bank shared internally. Across those papers, six question families repeat every year: (1) weight/ratio word puzzles, (2) speed-distance-time and train/tunnel puzzles, (3) probability and Bayes-style screening-test puzzles, (4) averages and work-rate puzzles, (5) classic logic puzzles (river-crossing, weighing, switches, jars, liar-truth-teller, Josephus/circle elimination), and (6) number series, coding-decoding, and cryptarithmetic/digit puzzles, with a handful of short lateral-thinking riddles and clock/calendar puzzles mixed in. Separately, e-con also runs a Hardware/Electronics objective round (diodes, flip-flops, op-amps) and a C-programming round (pointers, recursion, linked lists, bit manipulation) — those are NOT puzzle questions, so they are intentionally excluded here; ask if you'd like a predicted set for those rounds too.

Section 1 — Weight & Ratio Puzzles

Q3. A mixture of two metals weighs 24 g and is worth ₹204. If the weights of the two metals in the mixture are interchanged, it would be worth ₹180. If metal A costs ₹10 per gram, find the weight of metal B in the mixture.

Q27. You are at a desert fork with two guards, one of whom always tells the truth and the other who always lies (you don't know which is which). One path leads to safety, the other to danger. You may ask exactly ONE guard exactly ONE question. What should you ask?

Answer Key & Worked Solutions
Q1. Daughter = 56 lbs.
Working: Let s = daughter+dog. Father = 120+s. Total: 120+2s=260 → s=70. Dog=0.25×daughter → 1.25×daughter=70 → daughter=56 lbs (dog=14 lbs, father=190 lbs).
Q2. 64 kg.
Working: Weight scales with volume, i.e. the cube of the linear scale factor. Linear factor = 2 → weight factor = 2³ = 8. 8 kg × 8 = 64 kg.
Q3. 9 g.
Working: Let a, b be weights of metal A, B (a+b=24), price of B = p. 10a+pb=204; 10b+pa=180. Adding: (10+p)(24)=384 → p=6. Subtracting: (10−p)(a−b)=24 → 4(a−b)=24 → a−b=6. With a+b=24: a=15, b=9.
Q4. Daughter = 12, Man = 36.
Working: Let daughter's age 8 yrs ago = d, man's = 7d. Now: d+8 and 7d+8. 7d+8=3(d+8) → 4d=16 → d=4. Daughter now=12, man=36.
Q5. Birthday: 31st December; Rita is 41 today.
Working: For 'day before yesterday' and 'today' to differ by a whole extra year jump, today must be Jan 1 and the birthday Dec 31. Day before yesterday (Dec 30) = 40 (not yet had this year's birthday). Yesterday (Dec 31) she turned 41. Today (Jan 1) she is 41. By the end of this calendar year she turns 42, and 'next year' (the following calendar year) she turns 43.
Q6. Son = 10, Father = 40.
Working: f=4s. f+20=2(s+20) → 4s+20=2s+40 → s=10, f=40.
Q7. 3 minutes 36 seconds.
Working: Total distance = 300 m + 3300 m = 3600 m = 3.6 km. Speed = 60 km/hr = 1 km/min. Time = 3.6 min = 3 min 36 sec.
Q8. Train's speed = 3 × man's speed.
Working: Let tunnel length L, man's speed v, train speed u=rv, man is fraction f=1/3 through. General result for this classic puzzle: r = 1/(1−2f). With f=1/3: r = 1/(1−2/3) = 3.
Q9. 8:00 p.m.
Working: Net gain per hour cycle = 6 ft. Position before each hour's climb: 0,6,12,18,24,30,36,42,48,54,60,66 (after 11 full climb-slip cycles). On the 12th climb he goes from 66 to 66+18=84, exactly reaching the top. The 12th hour begins at 9:00 a.m. + 11 hours = 8:00 p.m.
Q10. 16 km/hr (relative to the train).
Working: Train covers 2 km at 8 km/hr → takes 15 min (0.25 hr). Athlete's relative speed u satisfies 2L/u = T (goes length L forward and L back relative to train) → u = 2(2)/0.25 = 16 km/hr.
Q11. 15 minutes.
Working: Head start distance = mother's rate × 9 min = 9D/40. Relative closing speed = D/25−D/40 = 3D/200 per min. Time = (9D/40) ÷ (3D/200) = 15 minutes.
Q12. 0.5 (50%).
Working: 40 min = four 10-min intervals. P(no ambulance in 40 min) = 1−0.9375 = 1/16 = p⁴ → p=1/2. P(ambulance in 10 min) = 1−(1/2) = 0.5.
Q13. ≈ 42.9% (3/7).
Working: P(D)=0.04, P(+|D)=0.9, P(+|¬D)=0.05. P(+) = 0.04×0.9 + 0.96×0.05 = 0.036+0.048 = 0.084. P(D|+) = 0.036/0.084 = 3/7 ≈ 0.4286.
Q14. 11/47 (≈ 0.234).
Working: P(same colour) = 4×C(12,2) / C(48,2) = 4×66 / 1128 = 264/1128 = 11/47.
Q15. 1/3.
Working: Equally likely outcomes given at least one boy: {BB, BG, GB} (GG excluded). Only 1 of these 3 equally likely outcomes is BB, so P = 1/3.
Q16. 31/66.
Working: P(both red)+P(both blue) = [C(5,2)+C(7,2)] / C(12,2) = (10+21)/66 = 31/66.
Q17. 147 runs.
Working: Let average after n innings = 20 (total 20n). After scoring 68: 20n+68=22(n+1) → n=23, so total after 24 innings = 22×24=528. For average 27 after 25 innings: total needed = 27×25=675. Runs needed = 675−528 = 147.
Q18. 15 days.
Working: Total work = 8×15×6 = 720 worker-hours. Days for 6 workers at 8 hrs/day: 720 ÷ (6×8) = 15 days.
Q19. 9.6 hours (9 hours 36 minutes).
Working: Let total time = T. Pipe A works (T−4) hrs, Pipe B works T hrs: (T−4)/12 + T/18 = 1. Multiply by 36: 3(T−4)+2T=36 → 5T=48 → T=9.6 hrs.
Q20. 58.
Working: Total marks = 40×62 = 2480. Top-5 total = 5×90 = 450. Remaining sum = 2030 over 35 students. Average = 2030/35 = 58.
Q21. 17 crossings.
Working: General formula for n adults with a 2-child/1-adult canoe: 2n+1 crossings. For n=8: 2×8+1 = 17. Pattern: both children cross → one child returns → one explorer crosses → the other child (who is now on the far side... ) returns; repeat, ending once the last explorer crosses (the final return trip isn't needed).
Q22. Take 1 coin from box 1, 2 coins from box 2, 3 from box 3, ... 12 from box 12, and weigh all 78 coins together.
Working: If all coins were genuine (10 g), total = 78×10 = 780 g. Each coin from the defective box is 1 g light, so the shortfall in grams below 780 g exactly equals the number of coins taken from the defective box — which equals that box's number. E.g. if the scale reads 771 g, the shortfall (9 g) means box 9 is defective.
Q23. Leave switch 4 off the whole time. Turn switch 1 on for several minutes, then off. Turn switch 2 on and leave it on. Turn switch 3 on only briefly right before entering, then off. Enter and check each bulb.
Working: Bulb states after entering: switch 2's bulb is ON. Switch 1's bulb is OFF but noticeably warm (was on a long time). Switch 3's bulb is OFF but only slightly warm (was on briefly). Switch 4's bulb is OFF and cold (never turned on). All four are now distinguishable.
Q24. Draw from the box labelled 'Pens & Pencils'.
Working: Since every label is wrong, this box cannot actually be mixed — it holds only pens or only pencils. Whatever single item you draw tells you its true (single) content, so relabel this box accordingly. The box labelled 'Pens' can't truly be Pens (wrong label) and, since the 'mixed' item is already assigned, it must be the true 'Pens & Pencils' box (it can't be the other single-fruit type either, by elimination) — similarly deduce the box labelled 'Pencils' is the true 'Pens' or 'Pencils' box, whichever remains.
Q25. Position 57.
Working: Josephus problem with every 2nd person eliminated: survivor = 2L+1, where L = n − 2^m and 2^m is the largest power of 2 ≤ n. For n=60: 2^m=32, L=28, survivor = 2(28)+1 = 57.
Q26. 2 weighings.
Working: A balance scale weighing gives 3 possible outcomes (left-heavy, right-heavy, balanced), and you already know the fake is lighter, so w weighings can distinguish up to 3^w coins. 3²=9 ≥ 8, so 2 weighings suffice (split 3-3-2 and narrow down).
Q27. Ask either guard: 'If I asked the OTHER guard which path leads to safety, what would he say?' Then take the OPPOSITE of the path he names.
Working: Whichever guard you ask, the truth-teller accurately reports the liar's (false) answer, and the liar lies about the truth-teller's (true) answer — both routes lead you to the same (wrong) path being named, so the safe path is always the opposite one.
Q28. Ask him: 'If I asked you whether the LEFT path leads to safety, would you say yes?' Take his answer at face value: 'yes' means go left, 'no' means go right.
Working: This is a self-referential double-negative question. A truth-teller answers honestly about the left path. A liar, forced to lie about what he'd lie about, is forced back to the truth in his final answer — so in both cases the literal answer he gives points to the genuinely safe path.
Q29. 1113213211, then 31131211131221.
Working: Each term describes the previous one aloud ("look-and-say"). 13112221 → read as 'one 1, one 3, two 1s, three 2s, one 1' → 1113213211. Reading THAT aloud: 'three 1s, one 3, one 2, one 1, one 3, one 2, two 1s' → 31131211131221.
Q30. 56.
Working: Differences: 4,6,8,10,12 (increasing by 2). Next difference = 14. 42+14 = 56.
Q31. 63.
Working: This is the series n²+2n. Differences: 5,7,9,11,13 (increasing by 2). Next difference = 15. 48+15 = 63.
Q32. 13.
Working: Two interleaved series: letters go backward by 2 (Z, X, V, T, …) and numbers go down by 4 (25, 21, 17, 13, …). The next term (after T) continues the numeric series: 13.
Q33. 19.
Working: Series of prime numbers: 2,3,5,7,11,13,17,19.
Q34. F.
Working: 4 places right of C → G. 3 places left of G → D. 2 places right of D → F.
Q35. HBSEFO.
Working: Each letter is shifted forward by 1 in the alphabet (F→G, L→M, etc.). Applying the same shift to G-A-R-D-E-N gives H-B-S-E-F-O.
Q36. 41.
Working: Code value = sum of alphabet positions. C(3)+O(15)+W(23) = 41.
Q37. Sister.
Working: The man's grandfather's only son is the man's own father. So the woman is 'my father's daughter' — i.e. the man's sister.
Q38. 2178 × 4 = 8712.
Working: This is a well-known cryptarithmetic identity: 2178 reversed is 8712, and 2178×4 = 8712 exactly.
Q39. 148877 (=53³).
Working: Digit sum of 148877 = 1+4+8+8+7+7 = 35. It is a perfect cube (53³) but NOT a perfect square, and it is less than 2,50,000 — so statements (ii) and (iii) are true and (i) is false, satisfying 'exactly two true'.
Q40. One valid arrangement: 9 − 8 + 76 + 54 − 32 + 1 = 100.
Working: (Multiple valid arrangements exist — e.g. 9+8+76+5+4−3+2−1=100 also works — any correct one should be accepted.)
Q41. 4 × 4 + 4 ÷ 4 = 17.
Working: 16 + 1 = 17, using exactly four 4's.
Q42. 301.
Working: LCM(2,3,4,5,6) = 60, so the number is of the form 60k+1. Need 60k+1 divisible by 7: testing k=0,1,2,3,4 fails; k=5 gives 301, and 301 ÷ 7 = 43 exactly. So 301 is the smallest such number.
Q43. A bank.
Working: A bank has branches (locations) but not the tree kind.
Q44. A keyboard.
Working: Keyboards have keys and a space bar, but no locks or physical rooms.
Q45. A postage stamp.
Working: A stamped letter/envelope can travel worldwide while the stamp itself sits in one corner of it.
Q46. A coffin.
Working: The maker/undertaker sells it, the buyer (or their family) never personally uses it, and the person who uses it (the deceased) is never aware of it.
Q47. A coin.
Working: Coins have a 'head' (heads side) and a 'tail' (tails side) but no body.
Q48. 9:15 a.m.
Working: Clock speed factor = 48/60 = 0.8 (loses 12 min every 60). Clock showed 5:00 a.m. = 300 clock-minutes after midnight. Real time elapsed = 300/0.8 = 375 min = 6h15m, so it stopped at real time 6:15 a.m. Adding the 3 real hours since it stopped: 6:15 + 3:00 = 9:15 a.m.
Q49. 16 times.
Working: 6 chimes span 5 equal gaps in 10 sec → each gap = 2 sec. In 30 sec there are 15 gaps → 15+1 = 16 chimes.
Q50. Friday.
Working: 100 mod 7 = 2. Wednesday + 2 days = Friday.
